I write research that takes the official numbers seriously enough to question them. My work primarily analyzes the U.S. domestic aviation market, as an economist looking for the elusive trends and quirks hiding inside aggregate statistics.

Three papers, written independently during my final year at UNH, examine the same industry from differing angles: how schedule padding inflates the DOT's on-time metric, and how hub consolidation has produced a market where 60% of routes operate as monopolies. Both rely on multi-million-row BTS datasets, and the kind of route-level analysis that aggregate findings often leave out.

01 May 2026

The Demand Impact of Bilateral Tensions

Effects of Political Uncertainty on U.S.–Canada Passenger Volume

A three-year panel built from BTS T-100 International Segment data documents a sharp, asymmetric collapse in U.S.–Canada air travel beginning in early 2025. The Canadian corridor declined by as much as 14.6% year-over-year while every other major international corridor held steady or grew. A load-factor diagnostic confirms demand fell first and supply cuts lagged — and a carrier-type decomposition shows Canadian airlines lost 2.4 million passengers while U.S. carriers held flat, isolating the sentiment shock to one side of the border.

02 May 2026

The Block Time Mirage

Disentangling Schedule Padding from Operational Reality

An analysis of 7.7 million 2025 flight records testing whether the DOT's 15-minute on-time threshold actually measures what passengers think it measures. Findings: 71% of U.S. flights are scheduled longer than they fly, padding varies systematically by carrier in ways that inflate published rankings, and a fixed-effects model controlling for airport, time, and seasonality reveals that carrier identity explains only 3.1% of delay variance, with other, more structural factors dominating everything else.

03 April 2026

The Cost of Consolidation

Hub Monopolies, Fare Convergence, and the Collapse of Competition

Using BTS DB1B and On-Time Performance data across 156 commercial airports, this paper argues Spirit Airlines' collapse was the predictable result of a market that is structurally far less competitive than deregulation promised. Findings: 60.5% of served domestic routes operate under monopoly conditions, three legacy carriers have established hub-based pricing territories, and 41 U.S. cities face complete carrier concentration on routes to both dominant Southeast hubs — with budget price floors routinely exceeding $200 on short regional flights.
